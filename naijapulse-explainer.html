Nigeria's News, Fully Accounted For

NaijaPulse

One newsroom relay, five jobs, zero blind spots
We collect Nigerian news automatically, group everything about one real event into a single story, and then show you whose voice is missing from the coverage — instead of letting you assume it's complete.
How it actually works
1
The Runner Ingestion
Every 15–30 minutes, a digital "kid" runs to 10 different Nigerian newsstands — Punch, Vanguard, ThisDay, Premium Times, and more — and grabs whatever's fresh, automatically. No human has to go looking.
Real run: collected articles on the Oyo schoolchildren rescue from 4 different outlets within minutes of each publishing.
2
The Translator Embedding
Two headlines can say the same thing in totally different words. The Translator reads each article and captures what it's actually about — not just the words — so differently-worded coverage of the same event gets recognized as the same event.
Real run: "Kidnapped Pupils Freed" and "Oyo Schoolchildren Regain Freedom" — worded nothing alike, correctly understood as the same story.
3
The Twin Spotter Duplicate detection
A lot of Nigerian news comes from one wire service and gets copy-pasted across five papers. The Twin Spotter catches when outlets are agreeing versus just wearing the same words in a different t-shirt — so five copies never get mistaken for five opinions.
Real run: verbatim wire-copy stories on 3 different outlets correctly flagged as one voice, not three.
4
The Sorter Clustering
Every new article gets filed into the right folder — the real-world event it belongs to — instead of piling up as a wall of near-duplicate headlines. One event, one folder, everybody who covered it in one place.
Real run: 232 articles correctly sorted into ~130 real stories — hand-checked accuracy: 95%.
5
The Referee Bias & blindspot detection
For every story, the Referee checks who's in the room — outlets that tend to side with government, against it, or stay neutral — and flags when only one side of the field showed up to cover something political.
Real run: caught an INEC election-deadline story with anti-government coverage present and pro-government coverage completely absent — a real blindspot. Also caught and fixed itself flagging World Cup football as "politically biased" — a football score isn't a government story.
The honest caveat, on purpose: right now the Referee can reliably spot when anti-government coverage dominates, because our current 10-source list doesn't yet include a high-volume pro-government-leaning outlet. That's not a flaw in the idea — it's a known, fixable data gap, and knowing exactly where your own blind spot is happens to be a very on-brand thing for a blindspot-detection product to be able to say about itself.
